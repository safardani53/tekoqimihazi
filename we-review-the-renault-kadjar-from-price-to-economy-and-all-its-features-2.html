We review the Renault Kadjar from price to economy and all its features

FOLLOWING on from the success of the Captur, Renault is back with a bigger and better compact SUV — the Kadjar.

Ever since the Nissan Qashqai burst onto the scene, creating the world's first crossover, every company has been busy constructing their own versions

.

More Motors

It's become a busy segment, and it's not even clear what to call these models any more - crossover, compact SUV or soft-roader.

Nissan should be seriously worried

And the Kadjar can easily be compared to the Qashqai as it shares the same platform but with its curvaceous body, bold styling, and amazing frugal engines, Nissan should be seriously worried.

It's available in a raft of colours and looks particularly fetching in the Cosmos blue that you see here.

The front is dominated by a large Renault logo and sweeping LED lights, the lines flow around the flared wheels arches to meet  a curvy rear end and LED lights

It's a very upmarket cabin with sporty instrument binnacles

The Kadjur has a sporty stance but the plastic arches and raised ride height point to its sense of adventure.

Standard equipment levels are really high with four trim options available, starting with the Expression+, Dynamique Nav, Dynamique rasi S Nav and Signature Nav.

The interior benefits from plenty of soft-touch material and overall, it's a very upmarket cabin with sporty instrument binnacles and digital dials.

There's some nice touches like the front passenger grab handle attached to the centre console.

The modern infotainment is as you'd expect, touchscreen but too many actions are needed for simple tasks like changing radio station.

For instance you have to press Media, then select Radio, then click again to finally get to move up or down the channel which means taking your eyes off the road for too long.

You can operate some of the functions via the steering wheel mounted controls but they are situated behind the wheel so it’s better to familiarise yourself with them before you set off.

It's a proper five-seater with great leg and headroom in the rear.

The boot offers 472 litres of space which rises to 1,478 litres with the rear seats folded and around the cabin you will find an extra 30-litres of cubby hole space.

There's plenty of engine choices too, a turbo-charged 130bhp 1.2-litre petrol and two diesels.

The diesel start with 1.5-litre produces 110bhp with 260Nms of torque and it's the model I'm tested the six-speed manual gearbox but you can also have a six-speed auto too.

This engine is also the most economical - if you go for the standard n 16" wheels it will return an incredible 74.3mpg on a combined cycle with only 99g/km of CO2.

My car had the fancy optional diamond cut 19" rims which look great but the economy takes a dip to 72,4 mpg and the CO2 goes up to 103 g/km — but still a seriously economical machine.

It doesn't hang about either, 0-62 mph takes 11.9 seconds and flat out the Kadjar can do 113 mph.For a bit more grunt, there's a more powerful 130 bhp diesel with 330 Nms of torque and it comes with a six speed manual only but is the only Kadjar available with an on-demand 4WD system.

With temperatures starting to plummet and more bad weather predicted, 4WD, extra power and grip makes a lot of sense and is a small price to pay for a reduced 58.8 mpg.Despite the raised ride height it's a rewarding car to drive.

Suspension set-up is soft but there's minimal body roll and the rear end stays level when cornering hard so you get the perfect balance between comfort and road holding.

If you fancy a Kadjar then get down to your local, no not the pub, your local Renault dealer for a test drive.

With the range starting at £17,995, you can be the star in a reasonably priced Kadjar.

Key Facts

  • What will it cost: From £17,995
  • Engine: 1.5-litre dCi turbo diesel
  • Power: 110 bhp
  • Torque: 260 Nms at 1,750rpm 0-62mph: 11.9seconds
  • Top Speed: 113 mph (182kph)
  • Economy: 74.3.8 mpg (3.8L/100km)
  • Emissions: 103 g/km
  • We like: Looks, smart interior, handling
  • We don't like: Infotainment has too many sub menus
