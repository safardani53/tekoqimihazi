Welsh ref who showed Billy Vunipola his only red card jokes he helped spawn a rugby monster

THE WELSH ref who showed Billy Vunipola his only red card has joked  he helped spawn a rugby monster.

England’s powerhouse No8 will be unleashed against Scotland on Saturday for his first taste of Six Nations action in 2017 after injury.

Billy, 24, is set to  join  brother Mako, 26, as the cornerstones of a Red Rose side chasing an amazing EIGHTEENTH win in a row.

And one Welshman at least will be proud to cheer on the pair.

David Parry-Jones sent off battering-ram back-row Billy when he was terrorising fellow teens at The Castle School in Thornbury, Gloucestershire.

And he grinned: “He made a rather high tackle and I thought the only way to teach him a lesson was to send him to the touchline.

“He apologised afterwards, though. I went to an RFU disciplinary conference a few years later and said I had done my bit to improve English discipline  by sending Billy Vunipola off!

“People laughed a lot but he’s not been in trouble since. I  like to think he saw the light from that red card.

“As he has gone on, he has come up against players bigger than him and  has had to moderate his attitude. And he is an amazing player for it.

“He used to  run through sides. He’d  run up to the tryline and wait for someone to come up to him, pass the ball to them and let them score a try.

“I am only sorry he did not decide to play for Wales instead of England.”

Billy and Mako could have both played for the Welsh  having left Tonga in 1998, when their dad Fe’ao was recruited by Jonah Lomu’s old agent to come and play for Pontypool.

From a young age it was clear the terrorising Tongan two were special talents. Not only were they twice the size of their team-mates — but they also had the skills to match.

The brothers would often shock and amaze watching parents as they tore it up at Thornbury RFC.

Their old PE teacher Lloyd Spacey at The Castle School was among those awestruck.

Spacey said: “When Billy came to us at 11 years old, he was doing things most adult rugby players would be doing. He would  do spin passes accurately over 40 yards.

“He kicked out of hand beautifully, took the conversions and penalties as his place-kicking was fantastic.

“Billy slotted a few drop-goals too. It all came easy to him. Once he kicked the ball from a restart, caught it again on the 22 and strolled over for a try.

“Mako was just as skilful. He played loosehead for the school but would reverse pass the ball 25 metres.

“Their skills stood out  as well as their work ethic and  their dad had a lot to do with that.”

Mum Singa is also a huge driving force behind the brothers’ success. But she admits still getting stick for her sons  wearing the Red Rose of England.

She said: “After training as a  Methodist minister in Cardiff there was a vacancy in Thornbury, so they decided to send me over the Bridge.

“That was the beginning of the ‘traitors’ name-calling from my friends back in Wales!

“Their grandfather was the one who put the dream into them, saying: ‘Next time I come over, you will be playing for England’. That is how it began.

“My husband knew all along that they had the talent. It was just the method of working hard.”

Both brothers have defied the odds to get fit after knee injuries and help  England hunt another  Grand Slam.

Explaining their dogged nature, prop Mako said: “Growing up, dad wouldn’t accept we were hurt in any game.

“If either of us go down in a game the other one will come over and say, ‘Get back up’.”

In two days it will be the Scots who the brothers will look to floor.
